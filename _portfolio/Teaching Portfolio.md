Teaching Philosophy
---

<font color="blue"> My teaching philosophy is rooted in cognitive and behavioural constructivism</font> (Stewart 2013), with a strong emphasis on active learning, inclusivity, and inquiry-based education. The cornerstone of teaching practice is influenced by Bransford et al (2000) findings, noting that students learn best when they are actively engaged in constructing their own understanding, particularly when theoretical knowledge is contextualised through real-world applications. I implement this philosophy using Socratic questioning, Think-Pair-Share strategies, and structured peer collaboration. For instance, in practical sessions, students work in small groups to conduct experiments such as flow through pipes or fluidised beds. I scaffold these sessions with pre-lab briefings that use analogies (e.g., fluid flow as traffic) to support meaning-making and reduce cognitive load.

My approach is theoretically underpinned by Piaget’s cognitive constructivism (Piaget 1970) and Vygotsky’s zone of proximal development (Vygotsky 1978). I use Socratic questioning to evaluate student understanding and to encourage metacognitive reflection (Plato 2005). This method is particularly effective in workshops, where students are asked to solve problems independently or in pairs before engaging in whole-class discussions. These discussions are guided by targeted questions such as “Why does pressure drop in a bending pipe?”, which prompts students to articulate their reasoning and engage with multiple perspectives (Maceiras 2025).

A key goal of my teaching is to foster student ownership of learning. This is achieved by connecting research with theory and practice. For example, I draw on my own research in environmental biotechnology to illustrate how fluid mechanical principles apply to real-world engineering challenges. This connection helps students see the relevance of their learning and motivates them to engage more deeply, as noted by Bruff (2009) and Johnson-Glauch et al. (2020). Students demonstrate ownership through independent inquiry during practical, self-directed problem-solving workshops and reflective writing in lab reports. These activities encourage them to take responsibility for their learning, apply theoretical knowledge in novel contexts, and develop a professional identity.

Peer review is embedded in my formative assessment strategy to promote collaborative learning and critical reflection. During practical sessions, students exchange draft reports and use structured rubrics to evaluate each other’s data analysis and interpretations. This process encourages them to critically assess both their own and their peers’ work, fostering evaluative judgement and communication skills. In workshops, students engage in peer feedback on problem-solving approaches, which helps them refine their reasoning and learn from diverse perspectives (Mandavgane 2020). To evaluate the effectiveness of peer review, I collect student reflections on the feedback process, monitor rubric-based peer scores for consistency, and assess the quality of feedback provided. These measures help ensure that peer review is constructive, equitable, and aligned with learning outcomes.

My teaching is also informed by scholarly literature and continuous reflection. I draw on the work of Bransford et al. (2000) and Mayer (2005) to design learning experiences that align with how people learn. For example, I use concept maps and Python-generated simulations to visualise complex phenomena, such as velocity profiles in pipe flow, which supports students with varied learning preferences. 
Inclusivity is a core value in my teaching. I make reasonable adjustments for students with specific needs, such as providing large-print handouts and using clear, concise language for non-native English speakers. I also adapt my pacing and explanations based on real-time feedback, ensuring that all students can participate meaningfully. My approach is guided by the principles of Universal Design for Learning (Rose & Meyer, 2002), which emphasise flexibility and responsiveness in teaching.

In reflecting on my teaching goals and effectiveness, I have found that active learning strategies, when combined with clear structure and inclusive practices, lead to high levels of student engagement and achievement. For example, over 90% of students completed practical tasks, and more than 50% were able to solve workshop problems independently before solutions were provided. These outcomes are supported by student feedback and mentor observations, which consistently highlight the clarity, relevance, and interactivity of my teaching.

Moreover, I adopt the Four Cs method in my teaching, which is instrumental in connecting (by being prepared and inclusive), organised explanations (communicating clearly), engaging activities (collaborating), and consolidating by pausing for reflections (Loes et al., 2016). This was validated through students' active participation (over 90% via in-class responses and peer-review feedback). The student-centred teaching was also ensured through Loes et al.’s (2016) ten indicators (e.g., clear explanations, effective examples) and Radmehr et al.’s (2020) approachable traits.

In conclusion, my teaching philosophy is characterised by a commitment to student-centred learning, scholarly reflection, and continuous improvement. I strive to create a learning environment that is intellectually rigorous, inclusive, and responsive to the diverse needs of students. By integrating theory, research, and practice, I aim to empower students to become critical thinkers, effective collaborators, and reflective practitioners.
